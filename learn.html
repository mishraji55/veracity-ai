Learn About Misinformation

Understanding different types of false information and how to spot them

Fake News

Fake news refers to completely fabricated stories designed to deceive readers. Unlike satire or opinion pieces, fake news is presented as factual journalism.

Common Characteristics:

  • False or misleading headlines
  • Unverifiable sources or attribution
  • Poor grammar and spelling
  • Sensational or clickbait language
  • Lack of bylines or author information

Example:

"Breaking: Celebrity announces retirement after secret illness nobody knew about! Doctors aren't talking!"

Notice the sensationalism, unverifiable claims, and urgency

Detection Tips:

  • Odd eye blinking patterns
  • Unnatural facial movements
  • Audio-video mismatches
  • Artifacts around edges

Deepfakes

Deepfakes are synthetic media created using artificial intelligence to make videos or audio of people saying or doing things they never did. These can be highly realistic and extremely damaging.

Why They're Dangerous:

  • Can manipulate public opinion and cause political instability
  • Difficult to distinguish from authentic content
  • Can cause severe reputational damage to individuals
  • Technology improves constantly, making them harder to detect

Manipulated Statistics

Statistics and data can be misrepresented through selective reporting, misleading visualizations, or cherry-picking data to support a false narrative.

Common Manipulation Tactics:

  • Misleading graphs with distorted scales
  • Cherry-picking data points that support a narrative
  • Correlation presented as causation
  • Missing context or sample sizes
  • Outdated data presented as current

Real-world Example:

"Study shows 85% increase in X!" - but it's from 20 people to 37 people out of millions

Always check the actual numbers, sample size, and source of statistics

Emotional Triggers Used:

Fear Outrage Urgency
Tribal Identity Hope/Promise

Emotional Manipulation

Misinformation often bypasses our critical thinking by triggering strong emotions. When we feel fear, anger, or outrage, we're less likely to verify information before sharing it.

Red Flags to Watch:

  • ALL CAPS TEXT or excessive punctuation!!!
  • Words like "everyone is talking about" or "they don't want you to know"
  • Share before it gets deleted" or "limited time"
  • Personal attacks on credibility of opposing views
  • Appeals to tribal identity or "us vs them" narratives

How to Spot Misinformation

Check Source

Verify the author and publication credibility

Verify Claims

Cross-check facts with reliable sources

Check Date

Ensure information is recent and relevant

Examine Evidence

Look for citations and supporting evidence

Pause

Take time before sharing emotional content
